Decoding Your Cash Flow Statement: A Simple Guide

Authored by Edz Lilian Galdones on September 2, 2025

Profit is great, but cash is king. You can have a profitable business on paper and still go bankrupt because you ran out of cash. This is why the Statement of Cash Flows is arguably the most critical financial report for any business owner. It shows you where your cash came from, where it went, and its net change over a period.

Let's break it down into its three core components.

1. Cash Flow from Operating Activities

This is the lifeblood of your business. It represents the cash generated from your primary business operations—selling your products or services. It starts with your net income and then adjusts for non-cash items (like depreciation) and changes in working capital (like accounts receivable and inventory). A healthy, positive number here means your core business is successfully generating more cash than it consumes.

What to look for: Is this number consistently positive and growing? A negative operating cash flow is a major warning sign that your business model may not be sustainable.

2. Cash Flow from Investing Activities

This section tracks the cash used to buy or sell long-term assets. Think of things like purchasing new equipment, buying a property, or even investing in other companies. For a growing company, this number is often negative, which isn't necessarily a bad thing—it shows you're investing in assets to fuel future growth. Conversely, if you sell off a major asset, you'll see a positive cash inflow here.

What to look for: Are you making strategic investments in your business's future? Or are you selling off assets just to cover operational shortfalls? The context is key.

3. Cash Flow from Financing Activities

This part of the statement shows how you're funding your business with outside capital. It includes cash inflows from taking out loans or issuing stock, and cash outflows from repaying debt, paying dividends, or buying back stock. For a startup, you'll likely see a positive inflow here from investor funding or a bank loan.

What to look for: How are you balancing debt and equity? Are you heavily reliant on loans to stay afloat, or are you in a strong enough position to pay down debt or reward shareholders?

Putting It All Together: The Bottom Line

The final line on the statement, "Net Increase/Decrease in Cash," tells you the story. It's the sum of the cash flows from all three activities. This number should directly correspond to the change in your cash balance on your Balance Sheet from the beginning to the end of the period.
